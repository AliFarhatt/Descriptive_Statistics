Types of Data 2 / 26
What kind of data are we dealing with?
Qualitative Data

Describes categories or labels — data that cannot be measured with numbers in a meaningful way.


Examples: colours, names, job titles, majors

Quantitative Data

Represents numerical values — data that can be counted or measured.


DISCRETE

Counted values — whole numbers only.
E.g. number of seats

CONTINUOUS

Measured values — any value in a range.
E.g. age, liquid volume

Identifying Data Types — Practice 3 / 26
Classify each example
Colours of automobiles on a used car lot
✓ Colour is a label, not a number — it cannot be measured or ordered meaningfully.
Numbers on the shirts of a girls' soccer team
✓ Shirt numbers are labels — you can't add or average them meaningfully. Qualitative.
Number of seats in a movie theater
✓ Counted in whole numbers — you can't have half a seat. Quantitative & Discrete.
Majors of students in a university
✓ A major is a category name (e.g. "Biology") — not a number. Qualitative.
Ages of a sample of 350 hospital employees
✓ Age can take any value (e.g. 34.7 years) — measured, not counted. Quantitative & Continuous.
Amount of liquid in different bottles in a science lab
✓ Volume is measured and can be any value (e.g. 45.3 mL). Quantitative & Continuous.
Sampling Methods 4 / 26
Four sampling techniques

When we cannot study an entire population, we select a sample. The method we use affects how representative the sample is.

Simple Random

Every member of the population has an equal chance of being selected.

Stratified

Population is divided into groups (strata); members are selected proportionally from each.

Systematic

Select every kth person from an ordered list or sequence.

Convenience

Select whoever is easiest to reach. Quick but can be biased.

Sampling Methods 5 / 26
Simple Random Sampling
Every member has an equal chance
  • 1
    Number every member of the population.
  • 2
    Use a random method (dice, random number table, computer) to select individuals.
  • 3
    Each person has the same probability of being chosen.
Key idea
No group is favoured. The sample reflects the population without deliberate structure.

Strength: Unbiased
Limitation: Requires a full list of the population
Sampling Methods 6 / 26
Systematic Sampling
Select every kth person
  • 1
    Order the population in a list.
  • 2
    Choose a starting point at random.
  • 3
    Select every kth member after that point.

k = Population size ÷ Sample size
Study guide example
"Every fifth person boarding a plane is searched thoroughly."

Here k = 5. The pattern repeats regularly through the queue.
Sampling Methods 7 / 26
Stratified Sampling
Sample proportionally from each group
  • 1
    Divide the population into distinct strata (groups).
  • 2
    Calculate each stratum's proportion of the total.
  • 3
    Multiply that proportion by the desired sample size.
  • 4
    Randomly select that many people from each stratum.
Study guide example
"A researcher randomly selects 50 male and 50 female teachers."

Gender is the stratum. Equal numbers are drawn from each group → Stratified sampling.
Sampling Methods 8 / 26
Convenience Sampling
Use whoever is easiest to reach

The researcher selects participants simply because they are available and accessible — not by a random or structured method.


Study guide example
"A college student interviews everyone in his biology class."

The class is used because it is the most convenient group to survey.
When is it a problem?

The sample may not reflect the wider population. The biology class might share traits (e.g. same major) that distort results.


Key identifier

No randomisation. Whoever is on hand is used.

Identifying Sampling Technique — Practice 9 / 26
Which method is being used?
Every fifth person boarding a plane is searched thoroughly.
✓ A fixed interval (every 5th person) is applied to an ordered sequence — that's Systematic sampling.
A researcher randomly selects and interviews fifty male and fifty female teachers.
✓ The population is split into two groups (male/female) and randomly sampled from each — that's Stratified sampling.
Based on 12,500 responses from 42,000 surveys sent to alumni, a university estimated the annual salary.
✓ Surveys were sent to all alumni and anyone could respond — each alumnus had an equal chance of being included. Simple Random (self-selected responses).
A community college student interviews everyone in his/her biology class to determine the percentage of students who own a car.
✓ The class was chosen simply because it was available — no randomisation. That's Convenience sampling.

Frequency Tables 11 / 26
Organising raw data

A frequency table lists each value (or category) alongside the number of times it appears in the data set.


  • Makes patterns easy to spot at a glance
  • Reduces large data sets to a compact summary
  • Foundation for histograms and graphs

Reading a frequency table
Frequency = count of how often a value appears
Total frequency = number of data points overall

Grouping continuous data into class intervals

When data covers a wide range, individual values are grouped into class intervals.


  • Each interval covers an equal width
  • Intervals do not overlap
  • All data must fit into one interval

Histograms 14 / 26
Representing grouped data visually
  • Each bar represents one class interval
  • Bar height = frequency of that interval
  • Bars are adjacent — no gaps (data is continuous)
  • The x-axis shows the measurement, y-axis shows frequency

Key distinction from bar charts
In a histogram, the area of each bar is proportional to frequency. Bars touch because there are no gaps in continuous data.

Cumulative Frequency 17 / 26
Building a running total

The cumulative frequency at any point is the total number of data values up to and including that class interval.


  • Each row adds its frequency to the previous cumulative total
  • The final cumulative frequency = total number of data points
  • Used to draw a cumulative frequency graph

Estimating the Median 19 / 26
Reading the median from the graph

The median is the middle value. In a data set of 300, it sits at the 150th value.


  • 1
    Find n/2 on the cumulative frequency axis: 300 ÷ 2 = 150
  • 2
    Draw a horizontal line across at 150.
  • 3
    Drop a vertical line down to the x-axis.
  • 4
    Read off the value → ≈ 192 pies
Estimating Percentages 20 / 26
What percentage of days did Kenny break even?
Break-even point: 176 pies
  • 1
    Locate 176 on the x-axis (pies sold).
  • 2
    Draw a vertical line up to the curve.
  • 3
    Read the cumulative frequency: ≈ 130
  • 4
    Days below 176: 130 days did NOT break even.
  • 5
    Days breaking even: 300 − 130 = 170 days
  • %
    Percentage: 170 ÷ 300 × 100 ≈ 56.7%
Values Between Two Numbers 21 / 26
Days with between 112 and 232 pies sold
  • 1
    Read cumulative frequency at x = 112: ≈ 20
  • 2
    Read cumulative frequency at x = 232: ≈ 225
  • 3
    Subtract: 225 − 20 = ≈ 205 days

Key principle
The difference between two cumulative frequency readings gives the count of values between those two points.
Percentiles 22 / 26
What is the 20th percentile?

The pth percentile is the value below which p% of the data falls.


  • 1
    Calculate 20% of 300 = 0.20 × 300 = 60
  • 2
    Find 60 on the cumulative frequency axis.
  • 3
    Draw a horizontal line to the curve.
  • 4
    Drop to x-axis → ≈ 148 pies

On 20% of days, fewer than 148 pies were sold.
Quartiles 23 / 26
Q₁, Q₃ and the interquartile range
Definitions

Q₁ (Lower Quartile) — 25th percentile → n/4 = 75
Q₃ (Upper Quartile) — 75th percentile → 3n/4 = 225
IQR = Q₃ − Q₁

  • Q₁
    Find 75 on y-axis → draw across → ≈ 155 pies
  • Q₃
    Find 225 on y-axis → draw across → ≈ 238 pies
  • IQR
    238 − 155 = ≈ 83 pies
Interquartile Range 24 / 26
Why does the IQR matter?

The interquartile range measures the spread of the middle 50% of the data.


  • It is not affected by extreme values (outliers)
  • A small IQR means the data is clustered tightly
  • A large IQR means the data is more spread out

In context
IQR ≈ 83 pies means the middle half of Kenny's daily sales ranged across about 83 pies — from ≈155 to ≈238. Sales are moderately spread.
